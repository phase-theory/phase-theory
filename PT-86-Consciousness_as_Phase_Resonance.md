Consciousness as Phase Resonance

⸻

Abstract

We explore the hypothesis that conscious experience corresponds to resonant, self-referential phase configurations within admissible phase structure. Without claiming that Phase Theory explains consciousness fully, we investigate whether known constraints—phase coherence, admissibility locking, saturation limits, and global consistency—can delimit the conditions under which subjective experience might arise. Consciousness is treated neither as fundamental nor as epiphenomenal, but as a high-order resonance phenomenon within phase dynamics. This paper explicitly marks the boundary between established Phase Theory and speculative extension.

1. Why Consciousness Is a Special Case

Consciousness presents unique challenges because it involves:
	•	subjectivity,
	•	first-person experience,
	•	self-reference,
	•	persistence across time.

Most physical theories:
	•	avoid the problem,
	•	or reduce it prematurely.

Phase Theory takes a different stance:

define what would have to be true for consciousness to be physical—without asserting that it is.

2. Constraints Any Physical Theory of Consciousness Must Satisfy

Any viable physical account must explain:
	1.	Unity of experience
	2.	Differentiation of contents
	3.	Temporal continuity
	4.	Boundedness (why not everything is conscious)
	5.	Non-duplication (no easy copying)

These are structural requirements, not psychological ones.

A phase resonance is a dynamically stable, self-reinforcing configuration of phase coherence that:
	•	persists over time,
	•	is internally differentiated,

	•	resists duplication.

Resonance is not vibration in space.

5. Consciousness as Resonant Closure (Hypothesis)

Hypothesis:
Consciousness corresponds to a closed, self-referential phase resonance whose internal phase structure both encodes information and constrains its own evolution.

Key features:
	•	self-stability (identity),
	•	internal modulation (experience),
	•	bounded interface (attention),
	•	saturation-limited bandwidth (working memory).

This is a mapping, not a proof.

6. Why Not Everything Is Conscious

In this framework, most systems lack consciousness because they:
	•	lack sufficient coherence depth,
	•	fail to form closed resonance loops,
	•	saturate too quickly,
	•	cannot maintain self-referential admissibility.

Consciousness would be rare and fragile, not ubiquitous.

No “binding problem” arises—binding is structural.
