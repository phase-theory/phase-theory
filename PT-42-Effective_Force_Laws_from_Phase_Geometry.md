Effective Force Laws from Phase Geometry

⸻

Abstract

We derive effective force laws in Phase Theory as emergent descriptions of phase geometry and admissible reconfiguration gradients, not as fundamental interactions mediated by fields or particles. Apparent forces arise when stable phase defects move along gradients in admissibility geometry, producing behavior well-approximated by classical force laws in appropriate regimes. Inverse-square scaling, central forces, and potential formulations are shown to emerge from geometric dilution and curvature of phase reconfiguration pathways. This paper replaces force ontology with a phase-geometric explanation consistent with all known phenomenology.

3. Definition of Phase Gradient

Definition 42.1 (Admissibility Gradient)

An admissibility gradient is the directional rate of change of the admissibility functional \mathcal{I}[\Phi] with respect to admissible phase deformation parameters corresponding to defect position.

Defects tend to evolve toward configurations of higher admissibility.

In regimes where admissible phase geometry varies smoothly, defect motion is well-approximated by motion along admissibility gradients.

\mathbf{F}_{\text{eff}} \propto \nabla \mathcal{I}

This is not fundamental—it is a local approximation.

Attraction or repulsion depends on:
	•	compatibility of defect topologies,
	•	whether proximity increases or decreases admissibility.

Like charges repel because:
	•	overlapping winding structures reduce admissibility.

Opposite charges attract because:
	•	combined topology reduces global phase stress.
