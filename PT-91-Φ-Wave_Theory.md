Abstract

Wave phenomena are traditionally described in terms of oscillatory amplitude and phase, with phase treated as a parameter characterizing wave position within a cycle. In this work, we reverse that hierarchy and propose that phase is ontologically primitive and that waves emerge as geometric flows of a fundamental phase field \Phi defined over spacetime. We formalize this framework by defining physical waves as gradients of a phase manifold, W_\mu = \partial_\mu \Phi, and demonstrate that classical wave equations arise as conservation laws of phase curvature. Interference is reinterpreted as topological compatibility of phase configurations rather than amplitude superposition. Quantum wavefunctions are shown to be exponentiated phase fields, with amplitude emerging from phase-density coarse-graining. Energy is identified with phase curvature density, and mass with stable topological phase vortices. We demonstrate recovery of classical wave mechanics, Fourier analysis, and Schrödinger dynamics as limiting cases. The framework yields experimentally testable predictions in extreme phase-topological regimes and proposes a geometric reinterpretation of quantum nonlocal correlations as global phase consistency constraints. Φ-Wave Theory extends Phase Theory by establishing phase not merely as a quantum descriptor but as the generative substrate from which all wave phenomena emerge.
