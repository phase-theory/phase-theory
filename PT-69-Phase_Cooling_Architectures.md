Phase Cooling Architectures

⸻

Abstract

We introduce phase cooling architectures as systems that reduce operational temperature and noise by directly exporting phase entropy and admissibility congestion, rather than by transporting heat to colder reservoirs. Without invoking refrigeration cycles, cryogens, or temperature gradients as primitives, we show that cooling can be achieved by controlled redirection of phase disorder into external admissibility sinks. Phase cooling enables room-temperature coherence, ultra-low-noise operation, and non-Carnot-limited thermal management for quantum, photonic, and hybrid systems.

1. Why Cooling Must Be Rewritten

Conventional cooling relies on:
	•	heat flow from hot to cold,
	•	compression–expansion cycles,
	•	entropy displacement via work.

These methods:
	•	scale poorly,
	•	require cold reservoirs,
	•	introduce vibration and noise,
	•	impose Carnot efficiency limits.

Phase Theory allows cooling before heat appears.

3. Phase Cooling Defined

Definition 69.1 (Phase Cooling)

Phase cooling is the reduction of effective temperature and noise in a system by redirecting phase entropy and admissibility congestion away from active regions, without requiring a colder thermal reservoir.

Cooling occurs when:
	•	phase distinguishability increases locally,
	•	entropy density decreases,
	•	coherent operation becomes stable.

4. Admissibility Sinks

Definition 69.2 (Admissibility Sink)

An admissibility sink is a region or subsystem engineered to:
	•	accept high phase entropy,
	•	dissipate admissibility congestion,
	•	remain functionally inert.

Sinks need not be cold.
They need only be phase-absorbent.

Local cooling arises because:
	•	entropy is removed structurally,
	•	not thermally transported.

	•	coupling fidelity,
