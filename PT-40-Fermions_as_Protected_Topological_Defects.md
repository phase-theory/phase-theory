Fermions as Protected Topological Defects

⸻

Abstract

We derive fermionic behavior in Phase Theory as the manifestation of protected topological phase defects rather than as intrinsic particle properties or field excitations. Without postulating fermions, anticommutation relations, exclusion principles, or spinorial fields as primitives, we show that fermionic statistics, individuality, and stability arise inevitably from nontrivial topological obstruction and phase holonomy. Fermions are shown to be structurally indivisible phase defects, whose protection enforces exclusion, identity persistence, and antisymmetric exchange behavior. This formulation completes the replacement of particle ontology with phase-native structure.

2. Topological Protection as the Key Distinction

Definition 40.1 (Protected Phase Defect)

A protected phase defect is an admissible phase configuration whose removal, duplication, or merging would require a topological transition that violates global admissibility.

Protection means:
	•	no continuous admissible deformation can erase the defect,
	•	no second identical defect can occupy the same admissible basin,
	•	identity is preserved under all admissible evolutions.

This is the essence of fermionic behavior.

4. Exclusion Principle from Topology

Theorem 40.1 (Topological Exclusion)

Two identical protected phase defects cannot occupy the same admissible configuration without violating admissibility.

Proof Sketch
	1.	Identical defects correspond to identical nontrivial topological obstructions.
	2.	Superposing them requires either overlap or annihilation.
	3.	Overlap trivializes topology; annihilation requires opposite winding.
	4.	Both are inadmissible for identical defects.

Therefore exclusion is unavoidable.

∎

This replaces Pauli’s exclusion principle as a postulate.
