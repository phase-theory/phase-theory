Structure Formation from Phase Defect Seeding

⸻

Abstract

We derive cosmic structure formation in Phase Theory as a natural consequence of phase defect seeding and admissibility amplification, rather than as the outcome of gravitational instability acting on random density fluctuations. Stable and metastable phase defects—both luminous and phase-silent—seed admissibility gradients that guide matter aggregation across scales. Filaments, halos, voids, and large-scale cosmic web morphology emerge as structural inevitabilities of phase-consistent evolution. This framework reproduces observed structure formation without invoking special dark matter properties, inflationary fine-tuning, or stochastic initial conditions.

Standard cosmology explains structure formation via:
	•	primordial density fluctuations,
	•	gravitational instability,
	•	dark matter scaffolding.

While phenomenologically successful, this picture:
	•	treats fluctuations as given,
	•	lacks structural necessity,
	•	depends on unseen substances.

Phase Theory reframes the question:

What kinds of phase structure naturally guide aggregation once ordering exists?

Definition 55.1 (Phase Defect Seeding)

Phase defect seeding is the process by which stable or metastable phase defects induce persistent admissibility gradients that bias subsequent phase reconfiguration and aggregation.

Seeds do not “pull” matter.
