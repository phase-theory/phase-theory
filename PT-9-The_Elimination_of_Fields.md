The Elimination of Fields: Gauge Structure as Phase Redundancy

⸻

Abstract

Fields are traditionally treated as fundamental mediators of physical interaction, occupying spacetime and carrying energy, momentum, and information. We demonstrate that within Phase Theory, fields are not ontological entities but effective, redundant descriptions of structured phase deformation. Gauge symmetries are shown to arise as invariances under phase reparameterization, and field equations emerge as coarse-grained consistency conditions on admissible phase configurations. This paper eliminates fields as primitives without loss of empirical content and resolves longstanding conceptual issues surrounding gauge freedom, vacuum structure, and field ontology.

⸻

1. The Field Ontology Problem

Modern physics is built atop fields.

Electromagnetic, weak, strong, and gravitational interactions are described as fields:
	•	defined at every point of spacetime,
	•	governed by local differential equations,
	•	endowed with gauge freedom.

Yet fields present deep conceptual problems:
	•	What is a field made of?
	•	Why are gauge degrees of freedom unphysical?
	•	Why do fields exist even in vacuum?
	•	Why must fields be quantized to describe particles?

Phase Theory addresses these questions by denying the premise.

Fields arise historically from attempts to describe:

Once phase is primary, fields become descriptive conveniences, not fundamental entities.

4. Gauge Freedom as Phase Redundancy

Definition 9.1 (Gauge Redundancy)

Gauge transformations correspond to reparameterizations of Φ that leave all admissible invariants unchanged.

Gauge freedom reflects:
	•	descriptive redundancy,
	•	not physical multiplicity.

This explains why:
	•	gauge choices have no observable effect,
	•	gauge fixing is required for calculation,
	•	gauge symmetry appears fundamental but is not ontological.

5. Field Equations as Consistency Conditions

Field equations (e.g., Maxwell, Yang–Mills) do not describe dynamics of real substances.

They encode:
	•	admissibility constraints,
	•	phase coherence requirements,
	•	stability conditions for defect interactions.

They are effective equations governing allowed phase configurations under coarse-graining.

6. The Vacuum Without Fields

In field theory, the vacuum is a seething entity with:
	•	zero-point energy,
	•	virtual particles,
	•	fluctuating fields.

In Phase Theory:
	•	the vacuum is simply a region of minimal phase deformation,
	•	fluctuations correspond to admissible phase noise,
	•	no ontological entity fills empty space.

This removes vacuum paradoxes without altering predictions.

7. Bosons Revisited

Gauge bosons are not particles in the same sense as fermions.

They correspond to:
	•	collective phase excitation modes,
	•	not localized topological defects.

Their apparent particle behavior arises only upon constrained interaction.

This clarifies why:
	•	bosons mediate interactions,
	•	they lack stable identity,
	•	they are not matter.
