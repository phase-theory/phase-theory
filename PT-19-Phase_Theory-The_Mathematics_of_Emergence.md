Abstract

We present Phase Theory, a mathematical framework in which emergence is not a heuristic phenomenon but a rigorously defined consequence of admissibility constraints on phase space. In contrast to standard physical theories—where emergence is treated as approximate, statistical, or epistemic—Phase Theory demonstrates that discreteness, causality, identity, locality, probability, and law-like behavior arise necessarily from global consistency conditions imposed on phase configurations. No wavefunctions, Hilbert spaces, state vectors, collapse postulates, or intrinsic randomness are assumed. Phase is taken as the sole primitive. All emergent structure arises from the mathematical requirement that not all phase trajectories are simultaneously admissible. This paper formalizes the admissibility functional, proves core emergence theorems, and establishes Phase Theory as a unifying mathematical substrate for physics, computation, and ontology.
