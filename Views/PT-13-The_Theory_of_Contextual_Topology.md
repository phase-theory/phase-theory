Abstract

This paper resolves the Quantum Measurement Problem without invoking wavefunction collapse, observers, consciousness, or probabilistic branching. Within Phase Theory, measurement is neither an act of observation nor a discontinuity in physical law. It is a topological interaction between a phase-structured system and a context-imposing boundary. Outcomes depend not on who observes, but on which constraints are imposed. We demonstrate that measurement is a process of contextual phase locking, where only phase configurations compatible with the measurement topology can persist. This framework explains contextuality, Bell violations, delayed-choice experiments, and the classical limit without contradiction or indeterminism.

1. The Measurement Problem Restated

Standard quantum mechanics faces an unresolved trilemma:
	1.	The wavefunction evolves deterministically (Schrödinger)
	2.	Measurement outcomes are discrete
	3.	Measurement appears to require an observer

Existing interpretations fail by sacrificing at least one:
	•	Copenhagen sacrifices determinism
	•	Many-Worlds sacrifices uniqueness
	•	Consciousness-based models sacrifice physics

Phase Theory rejects the premise that measurement is special.

3. The Contextual Topology Principle

Definition:
A measurement context is a macroscopic boundary condition that enforces a specific topological compatibility requirement on phase evolution.

Let:
	•	\Phi be a phase-configured system
	•	\mathcal{C} be a measurement context

Then:

\Phi \rightarrow \Phi' \quad \text{iff} \quad \Phi' \in \text{Topologically Admissible}(\mathcal{C})

No admissibility → no persistence.
