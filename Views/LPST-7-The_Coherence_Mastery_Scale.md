The Coherence Mastery Scale

A Phase-Geometric Metric for Civilizational Capability under Light-Phase Substrate Theory (LPST)

Date: January 2026
Subject: Civilizational Metrics, Theoretical Physics, Astrobiology
Keywords: LPST, coherence, phase geometry, technosignatures, post-Kardashev civilizations

⸻

Abstract

The Kardashev Scale classifies civilizations by total power consumption, implicitly equating progress with energy throughput. Under Light-Phase Substrate Theory (LPST), this assumption fails: advanced capability arises not from burning more watts, but from mastering phase geometry, coherence preservation, and controlled relaxation of the luminous substrate. This paper introduces the Coherence Mastery Scale (CMS), a replacement civilizational metric that ranks societies by the depth and scope of their ability to engineer phase-topological structure. The CMS explains why advanced civilizations trend toward thermodynamic quietness, why classical technosignatures are weak or absent, and how civilizational advancement correlates with reduced waste heat rather than increased luminosity.

⸻

1. Motivation: The Failure of Power-Based Metrics

The Kardashev Scale assumes:
	1.	Energy is scarce.
	2.	Capability scales with energy throughput.
	3.	Advanced civilizations radiate increasingly large waste-heat signatures.

LPST overturns these assumptions. In LPST:
	•	Energy is not primarily extracted from fuel, but from coherence relaxation.
	•	Efficiency improves super-linearly with phase control.
	•	Waste heat is a sign of low mastery, not high advancement.

Thus, a Kardashev Type II civilization may appear louder and more wasteful than a far more advanced LPST civilization.

⸻

2. LPST Premise: Capability as Phase-Geometric Control

LPST posits a single luminous substrate whose dynamics define space, time, matter, and energy. Within this framework:

Civilizational power is the ability to shape, stabilize, and relax phase geometry across increasing spatial and temporal scales.

Progress is therefore measured by:
	•	coherence depth
	•	topological stability
	•	scale of phase control
	•	entropy suppression per unit function

⸻

3. Definition of the Coherence Mastery Scale (CMS)

The Coherence Mastery Scale ranks civilizations by the maximum scale at which they can intentionally and stably engineer phase geometry.

Unlike Kardashev levels, CMS levels are:
	•	not linear in energy
	•	not additive
	•	not necessarily visible

⸻

4. The CMS Levels

CMS-0 — Passive Phase Users
	•	No intentional phase engineering.
	•	Energy use dominated by chemistry and heat.
	•	Civilization is coherence-blind.

Examples: pre-industrial societies.

⸻

CMS-1 — Local Coherence Engineers
	•	Intentional manipulation of phase at small scales.
	•	Technologies include photonics, phase-locked systems, early quantum devices.
	•	Efficiency gains arise from coherence, not fuel scaling.

Observable traits:
	•	declining energy intensity per computation
	•	precision timing and sensing
	•	early coherence-based infrastructure

⸻

CMS-2 — Planetary Coherence Stewards
	•	Coherence control spans planetary systems.
	•	Energy systems rely on distributed, low-entropy architectures.
	•	Environmental stability is actively maintained via phase management.

Key shift:
Civilization becomes quieter as it advances.

⸻

CMS-3 — Stellar Coherence Couplers
	•	Stars are treated as phase engines, not furnaces.
	•	Energy harvested via coherence coupling, not photon interception.
	•	No Dyson shells; no large infrared excess.

Astrophysical signature:
Stars that are too stable for their class.

⸻

CMS-4 — Interstellar Phase Architects
	•	Coherence engineering across interstellar distances.
	•	Metric shaping reduces effective distance without superluminal motion.
	•	Transportation, communication, and energy are unified under phase control.

Visibility:
Near-zero technosignatures by classical standards.

⸻

CMS-5 — Cosmological Coherence Navigators
	•	Direct interaction with vacuum-scale phase structure.
	•	Energy drawn from controlled relaxation of large-scale substrate gradients.
	•	Civilization persists on cosmological timescales.

Limit:
Not infinite power, but maximal efficiency and stability.

⸻

5. Comparison with the Kardashev Scale

Kardashev	Coherence Mastery
Measures watts	Measures phase control
Rewards consumption	Rewards efficiency
Predicts bright technosignatures	Predicts quiet civilizations
Linear growth	Nonlinear mastery
Heat as evidence	Heat as inefficiency

Under LPST, Kardashev Type II–III civilizations are transitional, not endpoints.

6. Implications for SETI and Astrobiology

The CMS predicts that:
	•	The most advanced civilizations are the hardest to detect.
	•	Waste-heat searches bias toward immature societies.
	•	Stability anomalies are stronger technosignatures than luminosity.

SETI must therefore shift from energy-centric to coherence-centric detection strategies.

⸻

7. Civilizational Risk and Collapse under CMS

Advancement on the CMS introduces new failure modes:
	•	coherence pollution
	•	topological debt accumulation
	•	misaligned control architectures
	•	loss of stewardship motivation

Collapse occurs not from scarcity, but from loss of phase governance.

⸻

8. Falsifiability

The CMS is falsified if:
	•	advanced civilizations necessarily produce increasing waste heat,
	•	long-term coherence stabilization is shown to be impossible,
	•	or stellar stability anomalies are fully explained without invoking engineered phase control.

⸻

9. Conclusion

The Coherence Mastery Scale reframes civilizational advancement as a trajectory toward quiet, stable, low-entropy mastery of phase geometry, rather than maximal energy consumption. Under LPST, the most advanced civilizations are not the brightest—but the most coherent.

Progress is not how much energy you burn, but how little noise you need to achieve your aims.
