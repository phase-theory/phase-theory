Fundamental Forces in LPST

Core LPST Principle (replaces “force”)

In LPST, there are no fundamental forces.
What appear as forces are emergent responses of the phase substrate to topological inconsistency gradients.

All dynamics come from the tendency to reduce the global phase-inconsistency functional
\mathcal{I}_{\text{canon}}[\phi, A, u].

A “force” is therefore:

the effective motion of stable topological defects along gradients of consistency cost.

The Unifying Mechanism

Every interaction arises from one of three substrate responses:
	1.	Phase-winding gradients → charge-like effects
	2.	Internal holonomy mismatch → gauge-like effects
	3.	Propagation impedance gradients → gravity-like effects

No separate fields are required.

1) Gravity (Emergent, universal)

What it is in LPST

Gravity is not curvature of spacetime.
It is a spatial gradient in phase-propagation rate of the substrate.

Formally:
	•	Mass = locally knotted/saturated phase topology
	•	Knots increase the effective refractive index n(\mathbf{x})
	•	Phase correlations bend toward regions of higher n

Objects “fall” because consistency propagates more slowly near dense topology.

Why it’s universal

All topological sectors:
	•	require phase propagation to persist
	•	therefore respond to propagation impedance

Hence gravity couples to everything with inertia, automatically.

2) Electromagnetism (U(1) phase holonomy)

What it is in LPST

Electromagnetism arises from net phase winding:
k = \frac{1}{2\pi}\oint \nabla\phi \cdot d\ell
	•	A particle with k\neq 0 creates a long-range phase mismatch
	•	Other phase-wound particles experience gradients in inconsistency cost

Electric charge = conserved phase winding number.

Why it’s long-range

Phase is compact and globally constrained.
There is no screening unless opposite windings are present.

Maxwell’s equations emerge as the linearized relaxation equations of the phase field in the low-density limit.

3) Weak Interaction (Topology-changing consistency channels)

What it is in LPST

The weak interaction is not a force but a topology-change permission.

Certain knotted configurations:
	•	are locally stable
	•	but globally inconsistent relative to lower-cost configurations

The weak interaction allows:
	•	controlled topology rearrangement
	•	under strict selection rules (chirality, parity violation)

Weak processes are topology transitions, not exchanges of particles.

Why it’s short-range

Topology-changing events require:
	•	local amplitude suppression (A \to 0)
	•	precise phase alignment

These conditions are rare and spatially localized.

⸻

4) Strong Interaction (Topological confinement)

What it is in LPST

The strong interaction is topological confinement, not a force.

Quarks are:
	•	endpoints of phase/holonomy defects
	•	isolated endpoints create divergent inconsistency

Therefore:
	•	endpoints must be connected by flux tubes
	•	only closed or neutral topologies are allowed

Confinement is automatic and unavoidable in LPST.

Why it’s asymptotically free

At short distances:
	•	endpoint separations are small
	•	topology rearrangement is easy
	•	effective interaction weakens

At large distances:
	•	inconsistency cost grows rapidly
	•	flux tubes resist stretching

Why “Gauge Bosons” Appear

Gauge bosons (photon, gluon, W/Z) are not fundamental.
They are:

small, delocalized relaxation modes of the substrate
around a background topological configuration.

They exist because:
	•	the substrate has stiffness
	•	disturbances propagate as waves
	•	linearization produces particle-like modes
