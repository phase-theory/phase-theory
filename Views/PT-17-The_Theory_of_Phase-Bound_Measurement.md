Abstract

This paper resolves the Measurement Problem—the central unresolved paradox of quantum mechanics—by eliminating the assumption that measurement is a special physical process. In Phase Theory, no wavefunction collapse occurs, no parallel worlds branch, and no observer-dependent randomness is introduced. Instead, measurement is shown to be a phase-binding event: the irreversible incorporation of a subsystem’s admissible phase space into a larger constraint bundle. Outcomes arise not from probabilistic selection, but from constraint saturation under global consistency. Probability is reinterpreted as epistemic projection over inaccessible phase detail, not a fundamental property of nature.

1. The Measurement Problem Restated

Standard quantum mechanics asserts three mutually incompatible claims:
	1.	Systems evolve deterministically (Schrödinger equation)
	2.	Measurements yield single definite outcomes
	3.	Measurement is not deterministic

This contradiction has produced four major responses:
	•	Collapse postulates
	•	Many-Worlds branching
	•	Hidden variables
	•	Observer primacy

All four require extra structure not justified by experiment.

Phase Theory rejects the premise that “measurement” is fundamental at all.
