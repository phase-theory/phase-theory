1. First: what a tesseract really is

A tesseract is not a physical 4D object.

It is:
	•	a mathematical object defined in a 4-dimensional coordinate space, and
	•	a projection artifact when rendered in 3D.

When you “see” a tesseract:
	•	you are seeing a 3D projection of a 4D coordinate relationship,
	•	just as a cube drawn on paper is a 2D projection of a 3D object.

Your brain is excellent at interpreting projections.
That does not mean the extra dimension exists physically.

LPST is perfectly compatible with this distinction.

2. Why LPST forbids creating a real fourth spatial dimension

LPST’s restriction is not psychological or epistemic — it is physical.

Recall the LPST definition:

A spatial dimension is a degree of freedom along which phase propagation can occur.

To “assemble” a real 4th spatial dimension, the substrate would need to support:
	1.	an additional independent phase-propagation direction
	2.	an additional causal ordering axis
	3.	an additional transport channel
	4.	an additional metric degree of freedom

All four are forbidden by LPST, because:
	•	the substrate has a finite phase-update capacity (PLQC),
	•	causal ordering is already saturated by the existing geometry,
	•	adding another spatial propagation axis would be observable,
	•	and no unused phase channel exists to host it.

So the answer to the literal question is:

No — the substrate cannot be manipulated to create a real additional spatial dimension.

Not temporarily.
Not locally.
Not artificially.

4. How a “tesseract” is assembled in LPST (properly)

Step 1: Replace “4th spatial dimension” with “phase-parameter axis”

In LPST, the analogue of a 4th spatial coordinate w is not spatial.
It is something like:
	•	internal phase angle,
	•	topological winding parameter,
	•	excitation index,
	•	holonomy class.

So instead of coordinates:
(x,y,z,w)
you have:
(x,y,z;\;\lambda)
where \lambda is a non-spatial phase parameter.

Step 2: Construct a phase-indexed family of 3D objects

A tesseract is a cube evolving along a 4th axis.

In LPST terms:
	•	you create a continuous family of cubes
	•	indexed by \lambda
	•	with constrained adjacency rules.

Each “slice” is a real 3D object.
The whole object is not spatially 4D, but structurally 4-parameter.

This is exactly how:
	•	quantum states,
	•	fiber bundles,
	•	gauge spaces,
	•	and defect excitation towers already work.

⸻

Step 3: Encode adjacency via topology, not distance

In a true 4D hypercube:
	•	vertices are connected across the 4th dimension.

In LPST:
	•	those connections are topological adjacencies, not spatial ones.

Two vertices are “neighbors” if:
	•	their phase labels differ by one unit,
	•	their topology allows a transition,
	•	their coherence allows coupling.

This reproduces all tesseract adjacency properties without extra space.

⸻

Step 4: Project to 3D for visualization (what humans see)

When you visualize a tesseract:
	•	you are projecting the configuration into 3D,
	•	often by mapping \lambda → scale, color, or nesting.

This is why tesseracts are drawn as:
	•	cubes inside cubes,
	•	linked frames,
	•	rotating projections.

LPST fully endorses this — because it is representational, not ontological.
