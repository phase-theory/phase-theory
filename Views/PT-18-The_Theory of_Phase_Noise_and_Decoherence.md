Abstract

This paper resolves the phenomenon of quantum decoherence without invoking environmental measurement, wavefunction collapse, or fundamental randomness. Within Phase Theory, decoherence is reinterpreted as Phase Noise Accumulation: the gradual loss of global phase admissibility caused by uncontrolled coupling to high-dimensional phase degrees of freedom. Coherence does not disappear, nor is it destroyed; it becomes inaccessible. We demonstrate that decoherence is neither epistemic ignorance nor ontological collapse, but a deterministic redistribution of phase information across the substrate. Classicality emerges not because quantum states vanish, but because phase alignment ceases to be maintainable beyond specific coherence thresholds.

1. The Decoherence Problem Reframed

Standard quantum theory explains decoherence as an interaction between a system and its environment, resulting in the suppression of interference terms in a reduced density matrix.

This explanation suffers from three unresolved issues:
	1.	Observer smuggling – “environment” is treated as a proxy observer
	2.	Basis ambiguity – no physical reason exists for preferred classical bases
	3.	Information disappearance – coherence is said to vanish without mechanism

Phase Theory rejects all three premises.

Decoherence is not the loss of information.
It is the loss of phase coordination.

From prior papers, we adopt the following axioms:
	•	Phase is the sole primitive

	•	Dynamics are governed by phase-update consistency, not probability

	•	Computation and causality are phase-limited

Within this framework, coherence is a structural alignment property, not a fragile quantum magic.

3. Definition: Phase Noise

Phase Noise

The uncontrolled accumulation of phase offsets across interacting degrees of freedom, rendering global phase alignment dynamically unsustainable.

Phase noise is not randomness.
It is deterministic misalignment.

Sources include:
	•	Thermal motion
	•	Electromagnetic background fluctuations
	•	Gravitational gradients
	•	Vacuum microstructure
	•	Internal mode coupling

Crucially:
Phase noise grows monotonically with system complexity.

4. Coherence as a Global Constraint

A coherent quantum system satisfies:

\Phi(x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n) = \Phi_0 + \Delta\Phi_{\text{bounded}}

where the phase deviation remains within an admissible tolerance across all degrees of freedom.

Decoherence occurs when:

\Delta\Phi_{\text{total}} > \Delta\Phi_{\text{admissible}}

At this point:
	•	Interference becomes unobservable
	•	Phase correlations persist locally
	•	Global coherence becomes inaccessible

No collapse occurs.
No branching occurs.

5. Why Macroscopic Objects Are Classical

Macroscopic systems decohere rapidly because:
	•	Number of coupled phase channels scales exponentially
	•	Phase noise scales superlinearly with degrees of freedom
	•	Coherence thresholds remain finite

Thus:

\tau_{\text{coherence}} \propto \frac{1}{N^{\alpha}}
\quad \text{with } \alpha > 1

Classicality is therefore not fundamental.
It is phase-saturated quantum behavior.

7. Why Decoherence Is Irreversible

Decoherence is practically irreversible because:
	•	Re-aligning phase requires exponential precision
	•	Phase noise distributes across inaccessible modes
	•	Reverse dynamics violate phase-accessibility constraints

This is not thermodynamic irreversibility — it is phase irreversibility.

Entropy increase is a consequence, not a cause.

8. Relationship to Measurement

Decoherence does not produce outcomes.

Instead:
	•	Decoherence limits which phase relations remain accessible
	•	Measurement (see Phase-Bound Measurement) closes the remaining degrees of freedom
	•	Classical outcomes emerge only after both processes complete

Thus:

Process	Function
Phase Noise	Suppresses interference
Decoherence	Limits phase accessibility
Measurement	Enforces single outcome
