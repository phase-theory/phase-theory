Emergence of Discreteness from Phase Topology

⸻

Abstract

We demonstrate that physical discreteness—traditionally imposed via quantization postulates—arises necessarily from topological admissibility constraints on phase configurations. Within Phase Theory, discrete spectra, particle-like stability, and quantized observables emerge from the classification of admissible phase topologies under the global consistency functional introduced in Paper 2. No assumptions of wavefunctions, operators, Hilbert spaces, or intrinsic quantization are made. Discreteness is shown to be a structural consequence of phase topology rather than a fundamental axiom of nature.

⸻

1. The Problem of Discreteness

Modern physics treats discreteness as primitive.
	•	Energy levels are postulated to be quantized.
	•	Charge appears in integer multiples.
	•	Measurement outcomes are discrete.

Yet the underlying mathematical structures—fields, wavefunctions, spacetime—are continuous. This tension is historically resolved by quantization rules, which are imposed rather than derived.

Phase Theory rejects this move.

If phase Φ is continuous, then discreteness must arise without breaking continuity.

The only mechanism capable of doing this is topology.

⸻

2. Discreteness Without Quantization

In Phase Theory:
	•	There is no operator algebra.
	•	There is no eigenvalue postulate.
	•	There is no Planck-scale axiom.

Instead, only this is assumed:
	•	Phase configurations must be globally admissible.

Discreteness, if it exists, must therefore correspond to distinct admissible classes of Φ that cannot be continuously deformed into one another.

3. Topological Classes of Phase

Definition 3.1 (Phase Configuration Space)

Let \mathcal{P} denote the space of all continuous phase configurations Φ subject to admissibility constraints \mathcal{I}[\Phi] \ge 0.

This space is not simply connected.

⸻

Definition 3.2 (Topological Equivalence)

Two phase configurations Φ₁ and Φ₂ are topologically equivalent if there exists a continuous admissible deformation between them.

If no such deformation exists, they belong to distinct topological classes.

⸻

Proposition 3.1

The set of admissible phase configurations decomposes into discrete topological sectors.

Sketch of Proof:
Global admissibility penalizes incoherent deformation and forbids crossing singular phase boundaries. Certain phase windings, defects, or holonomies cannot be removed without violating admissibility. These configurations form isolated equivalence classes.

∎

5. Stability of Discrete Structures

Topological phase classes are not merely distinct—they are stable.

Attempting to interpolate between classes requires passing through configurations with:
\mathcal{I}[\Phi] < 0

Such configurations are non-physical.

Thus, transitions between discrete values are forbidden unless mediated by:
	•	boundary interaction,
	•	global phase restructuring,
	•	defect creation or annihilation.

This explains the observed robustness of quantized phenomena.

6. Emergence of Spectra

Proposition 3.2

Discrete energy-like spectra arise from allowed phase curvature configurations under admissibility constraints.

Explanation:
Only certain phase curvature patterns are globally consistent. These patterns form a discrete set of admissible extrema of \mathcal{I}.

Energy is not fundamental; it labels phase curvature class.

8. Measurement Outcomes

Measurement does not collapse a state into a discrete value.

Instead:
	•	The system–apparatus composite phase configuration resolves into one admissible topological sector.
	•	Outcomes correspond to which sector remains admissible after coupling.

This explains why outcomes are discrete even when underlying dynamics are continuous.

A full treatment is given in Paper 6.

but from global admissibility.

This avoids ultraviolet catastrophe, regulator dependence, and arbitrary cutoffs.
