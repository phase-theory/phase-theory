Metric Geometry from Phase Gradient Structure

⸻

Abstract

We derive metric geometry in Phase Theory as an emergent structure arising from phase gradient relationships and admissibility curvature, not from a fundamental spacetime manifold. Without postulating distance, metric tensors, or geometric axioms, we show that effective notions of length, angle, curvature, and geodesic motion arise naturally from the way admissible phase configurations vary relative to one another. The spacetime metric appears as a second-order descriptor of phase gradient structure, placing geometry downstream of phase consistency rather than at the foundation of physics.

In conventional physics:
	•	spacetime geometry is assumed,

If spacetime emerges from phase order (Paper 10), then metric geometry must also emerge, or spacetime realism collapses.

2. Phase Adjacency as the Basis of Distance

Phase configurations are not embedded in space.
They are related by admissible adjacency.

Two configurations are “close” if:
	•	they differ by minimal admissible phase deformation,
	•	reconfiguration between them has low admissibility cost.

Distance is therefore operational, not absolute.

3. Definition of Phase Gradient

Definition 44.1 (Phase Gradient)

A phase gradient is the local rate of change of admissible phase configuration with respect to emergent ordering parameters corresponding to spatial or temporal distinctions.

Phase gradients encode:
	•	how rapidly admissibility changes,
	•	how costly deformation becomes,
	•	how structure resists reconfiguration.
