Abstract

Phase Manufacturing is introduced as a new class of production system in which goods are not fabricated through repetitive mechanical, thermal, or chemical processes, but are instead instantiated through admissible phase completion. Unlike classical manufacturing—which relies on tools that degrade, processes that drift, and factories that age—Phase Manufacturing defines production as the repeated authorization of identical admissible structures. This paper formalizes Phase Manufacturing, establishes its dependence on Phase Matter Routing and Assembly, Phase Mechanical Infrastructure, Phase Thermal Systems, and Causality Filters, and demonstrates why wear, calibration drift, yield loss, and throughput decay are structurally impossible. In Phase-Native Engineering, production does not consume factories; factories do not exist as consumable systems at all.
