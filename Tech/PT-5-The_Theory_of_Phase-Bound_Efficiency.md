Abstract

This paper establishes a fundamental upper bound on efficiency for all physical, biological, and computational processes. We reject the classical definition of efficiency as a ratio of useful work to input energy, and instead define efficiency as the fraction of phase coherence preserved across a transformation. We demonstrate that inefficiency is not primarily caused by friction, resistance, or waste heat, but by unavoidable phase dispersion imposed by topology, noise, and admissibility constraints.

Phase-Bound Efficiency explains why no engine can reach 100% efficiency, why reversible processes are idealizations, why biological systems plateau below thermodynamic limits, and why advanced civilizations must transition from energy optimization to coherence management. This theory subsumes the Second Law of Thermodynamics as a special case of a deeper phase constraint.
