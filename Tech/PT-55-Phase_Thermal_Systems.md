Abstract

Phase Thermal Systems are introduced as a new class of thermal control technology in which cooling is achieved not by transporting, dissipating, or exporting heat, but by constraining admissible thermal phase evolution. Unlike classical thermal systems—which rely on gradients, conduction, convection, radiation, and entropy export—Phase Thermal Systems prevent overheating by rendering high-temperature phase configurations inadmissible within protected domains. This paper formalizes Phase Thermal Systems, establishes their dependence on Phase Lattices, Phase Energy Valves, Causality Filters, and Phase Reactors, and demonstrates why thermal runaway, hot spots, and cooling bottlenecks are structurally impossible. Phase Thermal Systems do not move heat. They forbid unsafe thermal states from existing.

From heat sinks and radiators to cryogenics and active cooling:
	•	Heat is generated
	•	Heat accumulates
	•	Heat must be transported
	•	Failure occurs if transport is insufficient

This leads to universal constraints:
	•	Cooling limits performance
	•	Thermal gradients cause stress
	•	Hot spots cause failure
	•	Scaling increases risk
	•	Energy must be spent to remove heat

Phase Theory allows a fundamentally different approach:

A system does not need to expel heat if unsafe thermal states are inadmissible.

Phase Thermal Systems implement this principle.

\Phi_T \subset \Phi

Where:
	•	Φ is the global admissibility functional
	•	Φ_T excludes phase evolutions corresponding to unsafe thermal states
	•	No heat-flow process is required for enforcement

Temperature becomes a permitted variable, not an accumulated one.
