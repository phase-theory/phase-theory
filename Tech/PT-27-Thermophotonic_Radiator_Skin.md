Abstract

Thermophotonic Radiator Skin (TPRS) is a phase-structured, body-integrated radiative interface designed to export thermal energy and entropy from phase-native systems through controlled photonic emission rather than bulk conduction or forced convection. Unlike conventional radiators, TPRS operates as a differentiated tissue within Φ-Matter bodies, coupling directly to the Phase Thermal Interface (PTI) and Phase-Native Sink (Φ-Sink) stack. By shaping emissivity, spectral output, spatial distribution, and temporal modulation of infrared radiation, TPRS enables continuous, silent, low-noise heat export while preserving phase admissibility and global coherence. This paper defines TPRS, establishes its physical and phase-theoretic foundations, presents realizable architectures, and positions it as the baseline thermal exhaust organ of Phase-Synthetic Organisms and high-power phase-engineered systems.

1. Introduction

Every autonomous system that performs work must export entropy.
In compact, organism-scale systems, this requirement collides directly with constraints on mass, volume, noise, and geometry.

Traditional thermal solutions—heatsinks, fans, liquid radiators—assume:
	•	rigid structures,
	•	discrete components,
	•	externally mounted exchangers,
	•	tolerance for noise and bulk.

Phase-native systems violate all of these assumptions.

Thermophotonic Radiator Skin reframes radiative cooling as a structural, phase-aware tissue, rather than a peripheral device.

Radiative heat transfer:
	•	requires no moving parts,
	•	operates silently,
	•	couples directly to the environment,
	•	scales with surface area rather than volume.

	•	preserves form factor,
	•	avoids acoustic or mechanical signatures,
	•	remains available in vacuum, thin air, or sealed environments.

However, classical radiation is inefficient unless temperatures are high or surface area is large.

TPRS addresses this limitation by engineering the radiative process itself.
