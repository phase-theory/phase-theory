Abstract

Phase-Admissible Networks (PANs) are a class of distributed systems in which coordination, synchronization, and information transfer are governed by global phase admissibility rather than packet routing, clock synchronization, or probabilistic entanglement. In a PAN, nodes exchange and stabilize phase constraints instead of messages, and network state evolves through admissible phase configurations rather than explicit signaling protocols. PANs decouple coordination from bandwidth, enable low-energy global synchronization, and prevent nonlocal influence by enforcing locality of control within globally consistent phase structures. This paper defines Phase-Admissible Networks, formalizes their operating principles, describes their architecture and control mechanisms, and establishes their role as the connective tissue between Phase-Native Optical Processors, Φ-Load Controllers, Φ-Matter bodies, and Phase-Synthetic Organisms.

Modern networks move information objects:

	•	transactions

Coordination emerges only indirectly, through protocols layered atop transport. As networks scale, coordination becomes:
	•	bandwidth-limited

Biological systems do not operate this way. Muscle coordination, neural timing, and organism-level behavior are not achieved by message passing alone, but by shared phase relationships across distributed subsystems.

Phase-Admissible Networks formalize this principle for engineered systems.

4. Definition of a Phase-Admissible Network

A Phase-Admissible Network (PAN) is defined as:

A distributed system in which nodes maintain coordination by enforcing and relaxing shared phase constraints, such that global admissibility is preserved without requiring centralized control or nonlocal causal signaling.

4.1 Defining Properties

A PAN must exhibit:
	1.	Phase-based state representation
	2.	Global admissibility enforcement
	3.	Local control only
	4.	Passive persistence of coordination
	5.	Graceful degradation under load
	6.	Separation of correlation from communication
