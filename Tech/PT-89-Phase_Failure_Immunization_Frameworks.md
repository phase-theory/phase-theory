Abstract

Phase Failure Immunization Frameworks (PFIF) are introduced as meta-technological systems that preemptively eliminate entire classes of failure—including unknown, unmodeled, and unimaginable failures—by constraining admissibility itself rather than predicting behavior. Unlike classical reliability engineering, which attempts to enumerate risks, test edge cases, and add redundancy after the fact, Phase Failure Immunization operates by excluding any phase trajectory that could lead to systemic failure, regardless of whether that trajectory is known or understood. This paper formalizes Phase Failure Immunization Frameworks, establishes their dependence on Global Admissibility, Phase Design Proof Systems, Phase Compilers, Phase Technology Sandboxing, and Phase Engineering Ethics Toolchains, and demonstrates why catastrophic surprise becomes ontologically impossible. Failure is not mitigated.

It is non-expressible.

4. Definition of Phase Failure Immunization Frameworks

4.1 Formal Definition

A Phase Failure Immunization Framework (PFIF) is a global admissibility constraint system such that no admissible phase trajectory exists that results in systemic failure, regardless of whether the failure mode is known, modeled, or anticipated.

Formally, for a system S:

\forall \gamma \in \Gamma(S),\quad
\Phi(\gamma) = 1 \Rightarrow \neg \text{Failure}(\gamma)

If a trajectory could cause failure, then:

\Phi(\gamma) = 0

The trajectory does not exist.
