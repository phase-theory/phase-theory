Abstract

Force-Free Actuators are introduced as a new class of motion-producing systems in which movement arises not from applied forces, torques, or momentum exchange, but from admissible phase gradient realignment. Unlike classical actuators—which depend on force generation, reaction masses, friction, and energy dissipation—Force-Free Actuators produce motion by altering which spatial phase configurations are allowed to exist next. This paper formalizes Force-Free Actuators, establishes their dependence on Phase Lattices, Phase Defects, Phase Energy Valves, and Causality Filters, and demonstrates why stalling, wear, backlash, and efficiency loss are structurally impossible. Force-Free Actuators do not push matter through space; they permit matter to re-instantiate in a new allowed configuration.

4. Definition of Force-Free Actuators

4.1 Formal Definition

A Force-Free Actuator is a phase-native device that produces motion by modifying admissible phase topology such that an object’s current configuration becomes inadmissible while a target configuration becomes admissible, without applying force, torque, or momentum transfer.

Formally, the actuator defines:

\Phi_{pos}(x_1) \notin \Phi,\quad \Phi_{pos}(x_2) \in \Phi

Where:
	•	x_1 is the initial configuration
	•	x_2 is the target configuration
	•	Transition occurs without force mediation

Motion is topological reassignment, not acceleration.
