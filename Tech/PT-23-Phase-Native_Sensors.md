Abstract

Phase-Native Sensors (Φ-Sensors) are a class of sensing systems in which physical quantities are detected not by direct measurement of magnitude (voltage, displacement, temperature, intensity), but by observing how external phenomena perturb admissible phase structure within a phase-native substrate. Φ-Sensors encode information as phase shifts, coherence deformation, and admissibility stress rather than scalar signals. This approach enables ultra-low noise sensing, intrinsic sensor fusion, and graceful degradation under damage. Φ-Sensors integrate naturally with Φ-Matter bodies, Phase-Native Optical Processors, Phase-Admissible Networks, and Phase-Synthetic Organisms, functioning as the perceptual layer of phase-engineered systems. This paper defines Φ-Sensors, formalizes their operating principles, describes their architectures and operating modes, and establishes their role as the sensory foundation of Phase Engineering.

1. Introduction

Sensing is the primary interface between a system and reality.
Conventional sensors convert physical quantities into numbers:
	•	voltage from strain
	•	current from light
	•	resistance from temperature

These numbers are then interpreted by computation.

Biological organisms do not operate this way. Perception arises from distributed perturbations in coherent structures—mechanical, electrical, chemical—integrated across tissue rather than sampled at discrete points.

Phase-Native Sensors adopt this biological insight while remaining fully synthetic.

Instead of asking:

“What is the measured value?”

Φ-Sensors ask:

“How has admissible phase structure been deformed?”
