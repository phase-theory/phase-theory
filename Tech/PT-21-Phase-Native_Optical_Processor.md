Abstract

The Phase-Native Optical Processor (PNOP) is a computing architecture in which phase—not voltage levels, logic gates, or wavefunctions—is the primary computational primitive. PNOP performs computation through the controlled evolution of optical phase structures constrained by global admissibility rather than through discrete state transitions enforced by clocked logic. Information is encoded in stable phase configurations (Phibits), transformed by passive and active optical networks, and regulated by Φ-Load Controllers to preserve coherence and prevent admissibility failure. PNOP eliminates the need for continuous high-power switching, enables massive parallelism with low thermal load, and integrates naturally with Φ-Matter bodies and Phase-Synthetic Organisms as a physiological control and optimization substrate. This paper defines the principles, architecture, operating modes, control mechanisms, and engineering implications of the Phase-Native Optical Processor, positioning it as a foundational component of Phase Engineering.

1. Introduction

Conventional computing architectures are energy-intensive because they rely on forced state changes. Digital processors dissipate power continuously to maintain clocks, enforce logic levels, and synchronize discrete transitions. Even quantum processors, while exploiting superposition, remain bound to gate-based paradigms and fragile state preparation.

Phase Theory suggests a different computational regime:

Computation as constrained phase evolution rather than enforced state switching.

The Phase-Native Optical Processor (PNOP) realizes this regime physically. PNOP does not compute by toggling bits or collapsing wavefunctions. It computes by selecting and stabilizing admissible phase trajectories within an optical system.

9. Phase Buffers

Phase Buffers store phase coherence rather than energy.

Functions:
	•	absorb transient phase load
	•	smooth bursts of transformation
	•	prevent coherence collapse

They play a role analogous to capacitors and caches, but in phase space.
