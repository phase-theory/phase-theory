Abstract

Phase-Based Communications (Φ-Comms) is a communication paradigm in which information is conveyed by the establishment, modulation, and stabilization of admissible phase relationships rather than by the transmission of symbols, packets, or waveforms. In Φ-Comms, communication is realized as shared phase structure between endpoints, with meaning encoded in phase topology, coherence constraints, and admissibility transitions. This approach decouples coordination from bandwidth, enables ultra-low-energy synchronization, and prevents nonlocal causal influence while permitting global correlation. Φ-Comms forms the communication substrate for Phase-Admissible Networks, Phase-Native Optical Processors, Φ-Matter bodies, and Phase-Synthetic Organisms, providing a physiological analogue to nervous, endocrine, and proprioceptive signaling in synthetic systems. This paper defines Φ-Comms, formalizes its operating principles, describes its architecture and modes, and establishes its role within the Phase Engineering stack.

1. Introduction

All existing communication systems are signal-centric.

Whether electrical, optical, or quantum, communication is defined as:

The transmission of symbols across a channel.

Coordination emerges only after:
	•	encoding
	•	transport
	•	decoding
	•	interpretation

This paradigm imposes fundamental costs:
	•	bandwidth consumption
	•	latency accumulation
	•	synchronization overhead
	•	energy dissipation

Biological organisms do not rely exclusively on signaling in this sense. Hormonal gradients, neural phase locking, and mechanical coupling coordinate behavior without continuous message passing.

Phase-Based Communications generalizes this biological insight into an engineering discipline.
