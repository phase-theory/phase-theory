Abstract

Phase Communication is introduced as a phase-native coordination mechanism that enables information alignment without signals, carriers, transmission, or propagation. Unlike classical and quantum communication—which rely on energy-bearing messengers, probabilistic correlations, or channel capacity—Phase Communication operates by global admissibility alignment, allowing distributed systems to coordinate because only mutually consistent phase evolutions are permitted. This paper formalizes Phase Communication, establishes its dependence on Phase Lattices, Phase Clocks, and Causality Filters, and demonstrates how coordination occurs outside the categories of speed, distance, and bandwidth. Phase Communication is not faster-than-light; it is not transmission at all.

4. Definition of Phase Communication

4.1 Formal Definition

Phase Communication is the coordination of distributed systems achieved by embedding shared admissibility constraints such that only globally consistent phase evolutions may occur, without any exchange of signals or carriers.

Formally, let systems A and B share admissibility structure:

\Phi_{AB} \subset \Phi_A \times \Phi_B

Where:
	•	\Phi_{AB} excludes inconsistent joint evolutions
	•	No transmission term exists
	•	No timing or ordering signal is required

Coordination emerges because inconsistent joint states are inadmissible.
