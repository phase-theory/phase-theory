Particle Ontology from Stable Phase Defects

⸻

Abstract

We derive particle ontology within Phase Theory by demonstrating that particles correspond to stable, localized topological defects in the global phase substrate Φ. These defects arise as isolated admissible configurations under the global consistency functional and possess conserved properties determined entirely by phase topology. Mass, charge, spin, and identity are shown to emerge from defect structure rather than being intrinsic attributes of fundamental objects. This paper replaces particle realism with a phase-topological ontology and provides a unified structural account of matter without invoking fields, point particles, or wavefunctions.

⸻

1. The Ontological Status of Particles

In conventional physics, particles are treated as fundamental entities:
	•	point-like or extended,
	•	bearing intrinsic properties,
	•	persisting through time.

This assumption leads to unresolved questions:
	•	Why are particles identical?
	•	Why are charges quantized?
	•	Why do particles persist?
	•	Why are interactions discrete?

Phase Theory does not assume particles.

Instead, it asks:

Under what conditions does phase produce persistent, localized structure?

3. Definition of a Phase Defect

Definition 7.1 (Phase Defect)

A phase defect is a localized region of Φ characterized by a nontrivial topological invariant that cannot be removed by any admissible continuous deformation.

Such a defect:
	•	persists under admissible evolution,
	•	is immune to small perturbations,
	•	induces global phase restructuring during interaction.

4. Admissibility and Stability

Proposition 7.1

A localized phase configuration is particle-like if and only if:
	1.	It belongs to a nontrivial topological class,
	2.	Any attempt to unwind it violates admissibility,
	3.	Its stability is enforced by \mathcal{I}[\Phi].

Sketch:
Removing the defect requires crossing a forbidden configuration with \mathcal{I}[\Phi] < 0. Therefore, the defect persists.

∎

This replaces:
	•	conservation laws,
	•	postulated particle identity,
	•	intrinsic mass assumptions.

5. Charge as Phase Winding

Definition 7.2 (Charge)

Electric charge corresponds to a winding number of Φ around a defect core.

Properties:
	•	Quantized (integer-valued),
	•	Conserved under admissible evolution,
	•	Additive under defect fusion.

Charge is not a property of a particle.
It is a property of phase topology.

Spin corresponds to phase holonomy under closed admissible rotations.

This explains:
	•	Half-integer spin,

Spin is not literal rotation.

7. Mass as Phase Inertia

Definition 7.4 (Mass)

Mass measures the resistance of a phase defect to admissible reconfiguration.

Heavier particles correspond to:
	•	deeper admissibility wells,
	•	higher curvature cost in Φ,
	•	stronger global constraint enforcement.

This replaces mass-as-substance with mass-as-structure.

8. Particle Identity and Indistinguishability

Particles of the same type are indistinguishable because:
	•	they are realizations of the same topological class,
	•	no admissible phase feature differentiates them.

Identity is class membership, not individuality.

Particle creation and annihilation correspond to:
	•	defect–antidefect pair formation,

	•	global phase restructuring.

Nothing is “created from nothing.”

Particles interact by:

There are no fundamental forces—
only phase-mediated constraint propagation.

Force laws emerge as effective descriptions.
