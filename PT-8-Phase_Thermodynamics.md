Phase Thermodynamics: Entropy, Irreversibility, and Thermal Phenomena

⸻

Abstract

We derive thermodynamics within Phase Theory by identifying entropy, temperature, and irreversibility as statistical and structural properties of admissible phase configurations. Entropy is shown to measure phase dispersion over admissible configuration space, while temperature corresponds to phase noise density under coarse-graining. No thermodynamic quantity is treated as primitive. Irreversibility emerges from admissibility asymmetry rather than fundamental time directionality. This paper unifies thermodynamics with quantum and classical behavior under a single phase-based framework.

⸻

1. The Status of Thermodynamics

Thermodynamics is among the most empirically robust domains of physics, yet its foundations remain conceptually unsettled.

Standard assumptions include:
	•	entropy as a fundamental quantity,
	•	irreversibility as a basic fact,
	•	heat as a primitive form of energy transfer.

Phase Theory rejects these assumptions.

If phase Φ is the sole primitive, then thermodynamic behavior must emerge from statistical properties of admissible phase configurations.

From Paper 5, probability arises from ensembles of admissible phase configurations.

Thermodynamics arises when:
	•	the number of admissible configurations becomes astronomically large,

	•	microscopic phase detail is inaccessible.

Macroscopic thermodynamic quantities summarize phase configuration statistics, not microscopic laws.

3. Definition of Phase Entropy

Definition 8.1 (Phase Entropy)

Let \mathcal{E} denote the set of admissible phase configurations compatible with macroscopic constraints.

Define entropy:
S = k \ln |\mathcal{E}|

Entropy measures:
	•	phase dispersion,
	•	admissible configuration multiplicity,
	•	structural underdetermination.

Entropy is not disorder.
It is admissible phase abundance.

4. The Second Law Without Time Asymmetry

Proposition 8.1

For an isolated macroscopic system, entropy tends to increase.

Explanation:
Macroscopic constraints typically allow far more high-dispersion admissible phase configurations than low-dispersion ones. Admissible evolution therefore overwhelmingly favors expansion of |\mathcal{E}|.

No fundamental arrow of time is required.

Irreversibility is statistical inevitability, not dynamical law.

5. Temperature as Phase Noise Density

Definition 8.2 (Temperature)

Temperature measures the density of small-scale admissible phase fluctuations under fixed macroscopic constraints.

High temperature:
	•	many admissible micro-fluctuations,
	•	low coherence,
	•	high phase noise density.

Low temperature:
	•	restricted admissibility,
	•	strong coherence,
	•	suppressed phase variation.

Temperature is therefore a structural property, not a substance.

6. Heat as Incoherent Phase Transfer

Heat transfer corresponds to:
	•	redistribution of phase fluctuations,
	•	incoherent phase flow between systems,
	•	expansion of admissible phase space.

Heat is not energy carried by particles.
It is phase dispersion propagation.

7. Work and Coherent Phase Restructuring

Work differs from heat in that it involves:
	•	coherent phase reconfiguration,
	•	constraint-directed admissibility change,
	•	minimal phase dispersion.

This distinction emerges naturally without postulate.

8. Free Energy Reinterpreted

Free energy measures:
	•	usable phase coherence under constraints,
	•	the difference between total admissible structure and dispersed phase noise.

Systems evolve to minimize free energy because this corresponds to maximizing admissible phase stability.

9. Irreversibility and Phase Asymmetry

Irreversibility arises because:
	•	reversing macroscopic phase dispersion requires coordination across vast phase degrees of freedom,
	•	such coordinated configurations are overwhelmingly inadmissible.

This asymmetry replaces fundamental time-direction postulates.
